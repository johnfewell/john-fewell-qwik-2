# Sakura Fall

*An algorithmic philosophy for the Lucid 2026 portfolio background — second direction*

## The Movement

Sakura Fall is the computational aesthetics of *mono no aware* — the gentle ache of
transience. Where Lucid Drift dreamed in fields and trails, Sakura Fall observes
discrete, mortal things: petals released from an unseen tree, each one falling once
and never again. The background must behave like the last week of blossom season:
unhurried, inevitable, occasionally stirred to brief chaos by a gust, then calm
again. Nothing loops. Every petal's path is computed fresh from wind and chance,
so the fall the viewer watches has never happened before and will not happen twice.

## Process Over Product

Each petal is a tiny rigid body surrendered to three forces: gravity, a shared wind
field, and its own flutter. The wind is layered gradient noise read along the petal's
position and time, so neighboring petals lean together in gusts yet never move in
lockstep — coherence without choreography. Flutter is per-petal harmonic tumble: a
petal presents its face, then its edge, and its fall slows and quickens with that
turning, the way real petals stall when they catch the air. The renderer draws only
the present moment — no trails, no accumulation — because impermanence is the point.
Every constant governing this dance must read as physics, not preference: the mark of
a meticulously crafted algorithm refined through countless iterations by someone at
the absolute top of their field.

## The Gust

Calm is the default state; the gust is the event. A slow noise channel modulates
global wind strength, spending most of its time near stillness and occasionally
surging, sweeping petals sideways and spinning them hard before releasing them back
into drift. This temporal shape — long quiet punctuated by brief turbulence — is what
separates a living sky from a screensaver, and tuning its rhythm is where the
painstaking optimization shows.

## Depth and Palette

Petals exist at depths. Distant petals are small, slow, and faint; near petals are
large, quick, and vivid — three or four implied parallax planes from one continuous
depth parameter. The palette is blossom against the site's own sky: warm pinks and
whites over the daylight gradient, and for dark mode, *yozakura* — night-viewing
blossoms, moonlit silver-pink against the indigo. The petal silhouette itself is the
sakura signature: a soft oval with the notched tip, drawn with curves, never sprites.

## Seeded Variation

One integer seeds everything: the wind lattice, each petal's birth position, depth,
tumble rate, and hue. Seed 1147 is always the same falling; its neighbor is always a
different one. The tunable space — population, fall speed, wind strength, gust
rhythm, flutter, petal size — is the instrument; the seed is the score.

## The Conceptual Seed

The quiet reference is hanami: the Japanese practice of gathering to watch blossoms
fall precisely because they are falling. A portfolio is itself a record of seasons of
work, each shipped and let go. Visitors who know will feel the elegy; everyone else
simply sees petals drifting past a name, and that is enough.
